# ᨒᨚᨄᨗ — lopi

## The Name

The Konjo are a people of South Sulawesi, Indonesia — coastal, seafaring, and relentless. Settled along the shores of Bulukumba in the southernmost reach of the peninsula, they have spent centuries doing one thing better than almost anyone on earth: building boats that survive the sea.

Their craft, the *pinisi* — the great twin-masted sailing vessel that once threaded the Banda Sea and the Strait of Malacca — was laid down in Konjo shipyards with hand tools and inherited memory. No blueprints. The measurements lived in the bodies of the builders. The curve of the hull was felt before it was cut. When a Konjo master shipwright finished a keel, he knew it would hold. He had to. Open water doesn't negotiate.

The word for boat in the Konjo language is *lopi* (ᨒᨚᨄᨗ in Lontara, the ancient script of South Sulawesi). Not a warship. Not a vessel of conquest. A working craft — load it, sail it, trust it, bring it home. A *lopi* carries what matters across water that wants to swallow it whole. It is built to finish the crossing.

## Why LOPI

This software carries the same obligation. A task arrives — a failing test, a CI red, a message from your phone at midnight — and LOPI loads it into an agent, isolates it on a branch, and sets it sailing. The water is distributed compute, concurrent git branches, flaky tests, and the occasional hallucination. The *lopi* weathers all of it, scores what it delivers, and rolls back cleanly when the hull takes on water.

It is not a system that tries. It is a system that finishes the crossing.
